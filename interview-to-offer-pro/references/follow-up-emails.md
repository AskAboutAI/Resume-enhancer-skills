# Follow-Up Email Templates Reference

Three email templates for different stages of the job search process.
Each has a hard word limit. None use em dashes or en dashes. All follow
the shared writing rules from SKILL.md — no filler, no desperation,
nothing that reads like a template.

---

## Hard Rules for All Three Emails

Before outputting any email, check:
- Word count is AT or UNDER the limit (count before outputting, not after)
- Zero em dashes (—) anywhere in the text
- Zero en dashes (–) anywhere in the text
- No ellipsis (...) used stylistically
- No opener like "I hope this finds you well" or "Just following up"
- No subject line that says "Following up on my application"
- The email references something SPECIFIC — a detail from the JD, a name,
  a moment from the interview. Generic emails go unread.
- Tone is confident, not grateful or desperate

---

## Email 1: Post-Application Nudge (3 days after applying)

**When to send:** 3 days after submitting the application, if no acknowledgment.
**Word limit:** 60 words. Not 61. Count.
**Purpose:** Get eyes on the resume. Signal genuine interest without pestering.

Rules specific to this email:
- Reference ONE specific detail from the job description — not "the role" generically
- Restate fit in exactly one sentence — the strongest alignment point
- Subject line must be specific enough that the hiring manager knows which role

Format:
```
EMAIL 1: POST-APPLICATION
Subject: [Role] Application, [One specific thing that connects you to them]

[Body — under 60 words]

Opening: No "I hope this email finds you well." Start with something
         that shows you read the JD, not just the title.
Middle:  One sentence restating your strongest fit for this specific role.
Close:   A low-friction ask — not "please review my resume."
         Something like "Happy to send anything else that would be helpful."

[First name only]
```

Example structure (not a template to copy):
> "[Specific requirement from JD] caught my attention because [one-line
> reason you're a match]. I applied [X] days ago for the [Role] position
> and wanted to make sure my application reached the right person. I'd be
> glad to send anything additional that would help. [Name]"

## Email 2: Post-Interview Thank You (within 24 hours)

**When to send:** Within 24 hours of the interview. Same day is better.
**Word limit:** 100 words. Not 101. Count.
**Purpose:** Reinforce fit, reference a specific moment, stay top of mind.

Rules specific to this email:
- Address them by FIRST NAME. "Hi Sarah" not "Dear Ms. Sharma."
- Reference ONE specific moment from the conversation — not "our discussion"
  generically. A specific question they asked, a project they mentioned,
  something you laughed about. This proves you were present.
- Reinforce the STRONGEST example you gave in the interview — one sentence
- Do not say "thank you for your time" as the opening line. It buries
  the important content at the back. Lead with the specific moment.
- No filler close like "I look forward to hearing from you." End on
  something that adds value or shows forward momentum.

Format:
```
EMAIL 2: POST-INTERVIEW
Subject: [Something specific from the interview, not "Thank you for your time"]

[Body — under 100 words]

Opening: The specific moment from the conversation. "Your question about
         [X] stuck with me because..." or "[Topic we discussed]. I've been
         thinking about it since I left."
Middle:  Thank them by name. Reinforce your strongest example in one sentence.
Close:   One forward-looking sentence. Not a vague "looking forward to next steps."
         Something specific about the role or company.

[First name]
```

## Email 3: The Nudge (7 days after interview, no response)

**When to send:** 7 days after the interview if there has been no response.
**Word limit:** 80 words. Not 81. Count.
**Purpose:** Stay top of mind, add a piece of value, give them an easy out.

Rules specific to this email:
- Add a piece of genuine value. Not a random link. Something connected to
  what you discussed in the interview — a relevant article, an industry stat,
  a quick thought on something they raised. If you cannot think of something
  genuinely useful, skip this email.
- Give them an easy out. "If the timeline has shifted, totally understand"
  is the exact phrase from the original plan — use it or a close variant.
  This removes the pressure and often generates a reply.
- Do not say "just checking in." This signals you have nothing to add.
  The piece of value is what earns the send.
- Do not be apologetic. This email is a service — you're adding value,
  not begging for attention.

Format:
```
EMAIL 3: THE NUDGE
Subject: [Something from your conversation] + one update

[Body — under 80 words]

Opening: The piece of value. Lead with it. "Came across [article/stat/thought]
         related to what we discussed about [specific topic] — [one sentence
         of actual insight, not just a link]."
Middle:  Brief restatement of interest in one sentence.
Close:   The easy out. "If the timeline has shifted, totally understand.
         Either way, I appreciated the conversation."

[First name]
```

## Output Format

Generate all three emails in one block when requested, clearly labeled.
After generating, count words for each and confirm the count in brackets
next to the label.

```
EMAIL 1 — POST-APPLICATION [XX words]
Subject: ...
[Body]

────────────────────────────────────
EMAIL 2 — POST-INTERVIEW [XX words]
Subject: ...
[Body]

────────────────────────────────────
EMAIL 3 — THE NUDGE [XX words]
Subject: ...
[Body]
```

If any email is over the word limit, do not output it. Rewrite it until
it fits. The limit is a discipline, not a suggestion — shorter emails get
read; long ones get archived.
