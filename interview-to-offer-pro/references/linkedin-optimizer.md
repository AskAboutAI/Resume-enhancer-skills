# LinkedIn Profile Optimizer Reference

Rewrites all five key LinkedIn sections to attract recruiters for a target role.
The goal is inbound recruiter interest — not just a resume copy-paste.
LinkedIn copy is more conversational than resume copy and needs to account
for how recruiters actually search and scan.

---

## How Recruiters Use LinkedIn (Read This Before Writing Anything)

Recruiters search by keyword. Your headline is the first thing they see in
search results. If it doesn't match what they searched, they don't click.

Once they click, they read the headline again, then the About section's
first two lines (the rest is hidden behind "see more"). If those don't hook
them in 3 seconds, they move on.

Your job is to clear two gates: the keyword search and the 3-second scroll test.
Everything below is designed around those two gates.

---

## Section 1: Headline

**Limit:** 220 characters per version. Hard limit — LinkedIn truncates beyond this.
**Output:** 3 versions, each taking a different angle.

Rules:
- Use keywords recruiters actually search. Not your job title at your last
  company — the SEARCH TERM they type. Check job postings for exact language.
- Show outcome or specialty alongside the keyword. "Content Strategist" is
  a job. "AI Content Strategist | Built pipelines that cut production cost by 60%"
  is a signal.
- Do not start with "I am" or "Helping companies..." — that is Everyone's headline.
- No buzzwords: "passionate", "results-driven", "dynamic", "thought leader".

Format:
```
HEADLINE OPTIONS (pick one, customize before publishing)

Version 1 — Keyword-first:
[Primary keyword | Specific specialty | Metric or outcome]
[Character count: XX/220]

Version 2 — Outcome-first:
[Result or impact | What you do to achieve it | Who you do it for]
[Character count: XX/220]

Version 3 — Role-targeted:
[Target role title as recruiters search it | Niche angle | Tool or method]
[Character count: XX/220]
```

## Section 2: About Section

**Limit:** 3 paragraphs maximum. LinkedIn shows ~265 characters before "see more."
The first sentence must work as a standalone hook for the preview.

Rules:
- First sentence: no "I am a passionate..." opener. Start with something
  that makes a recruiter stop. A result, a provocative claim, a specific
  niche. Something that proves you are not the same as every other profile.
- Paragraph 2: 2-3 specific proof points. Use numbers. What you have built,
  grown, reduced, or launched. Treat it like the summary section of a resume
  but slightly more conversational.
- Paragraph 3: Clear call to action. What kinds of roles or conversations
  the user is open to. Be specific — "remote AI content roles at B2B SaaS
  companies" is useful to a recruiter. "Exciting new opportunities" is not.
- Apply all shared writing rules: no em dashes, no en dashes, no buzzwords.

Format:
```
ABOUT SECTION

[Paragraph 1 — Hook. The first sentence must work as a preview.
Must NOT start with "I am a" or "I help" or "Passionate about".]

[Paragraph 2 — Proof. 2-3 sentences with specific results and numbers.
More conversational than a resume but still metrics-driven.]

[Paragraph 3 — CTA. One or two sentences. What roles or conversations
you are open to. Specific enough that a recruiter knows if you're relevant
to what they're searching for.]
```

## Section 3: Experience Bullets

Rewrite experience bullets from the resume using the same XYZ formula but
with two differences from the resume version:

1. **More conversational:** Slightly more human voice. The resume is a scan
   document; LinkedIn is read by a person who already clicked on you. You
   can afford one extra clause that shows thinking, not just output.

2. **Add one learning line per role:** After the accomplishment bullets,
   add one sentence starting with "What I learned:" or "This taught me:".
   This line signals self-awareness and growth — two things every hiring
   manager values and almost no candidate includes.

Format per role:
```
[JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY] | [Dates]

[Bullet 1 — XYZ formula: biggest impact, first]
[Bullet 2 — XYZ formula: second strongest result]
[Bullet 3 — XYZ formula: third result or skill-specific proof]

What I learned: [One sentence. A principle, a method, or a shift in how
you approach the work. Not "I learned a lot." Something specific.]
```

Keep bullets to 1-2 lines each. LinkedIn wraps aggressively on mobile.

## Section 4: Skills Section

**Output:** Top 5 skills to pin, in order from most searchable to least.

Rules:
- Skills are searchable — recruiters filter by them. Use the exact terms
  that appear in the target job descriptions, not paraphrases.
- Pin the 5 most searched. LinkedIn allows 50 skills but only 3 pin to
  the top without "show more." The top 3 matter most — the rest are SEO.
- Prioritize: tool names over generic skills, specific platforms over broad
  categories, skills that match the job description keyword for keyword.

Format:
```
SKILLS TO PIN (in order — top 3 are visible without clicking)

1. [Skill — most searched for target role]
2. [Skill]
3. [Skill]
4. [Skill]
5. [Skill]

Searchability note: [One sentence on why this ordering was chosen —
which skills are highest-traffic in the target role's JDs.]
```

## Section 5: Featured Section

**Output:** 2-3 concrete suggestions for what to add.

The Featured section is prime real estate on LinkedIn. Most people leave
it empty or add their company's homepage. Recruiters who click on a profile
and see something in Featured are more likely to spend another 30 seconds.

Suggestions must be specific to the user's actual work — not generic ideas.
Draw from: their resume projects, any public-facing work, GitHub, tools they've
built, content they've created, talks or presentations, portfolio pieces.

Format:
```
FEATURED SECTION SUGGESTIONS

1. [Specific suggestion — what to add and why it stops a recruiter]
   How to add it: [One sentence — link, upload, or article]

2. [Specific suggestion]
   How to add it: [One sentence]

3. [Specific suggestion — optional if two is enough]
   How to add it: [One sentence]

If none of these exist yet: [Suggest one thing the user could create quickly
that would be worth featuring — a short LinkedIn article, a carousel post,
a short video walkthrough of a project. Give a specific topic, not just
"create content."]
```

---

## Delivery Format

Output all five sections in one complete block, in order. After each section,
include a brief note on what will make the most difference for recruiter
visibility — something the user might not have considered.

Do not add sections the user didn't ask for. Do not rewrite their entire
profile if they only asked about the headline. Match the scope to the request,
then offer to expand.
